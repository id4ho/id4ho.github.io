Categories: programming Tags: nand2tetris

NAND to Tetris

My first programming course was taught in Java. I often got distracted during trying to imagine how Java worked under the hood. Type <code> and you get <output>. Wait, how? What happens when I declare a variable? and why do I need that static word up there? How does a text file get translated into something the computer can use? And then after translation.. how does the computer actually process that generated file?

I didn’t have enough spare time to get into hardware/EE or even lower level programming while at school (I wasn’t actually a CS major), so I wound up accepting the magical computer on faith so I could move on with the assignments. Since then, I’ve learned a lot about programming and the different types of memory, some about compilation and bit about assembly (thanks primarily to the microcorruption ctf, which I highly recommend). These experiences have helped satisfy some of the questions I had when first learning Java years ago, but my understanding of underlying architecture, logic gates, and CPUs could be a lot stronger.

Toward building an understanding of how computers work from first principles I’m working through a course called NAND to Tetris. Since transitioning from not understanding a concept to understanding it often wipes your memory of exactly how you were confused in the first place, I plan to write some posts as I work through the course. In the posts, I’ll include what I found to be most interesting each week and what big questions were raised or remain because we tend to forget these things in retrospect.
