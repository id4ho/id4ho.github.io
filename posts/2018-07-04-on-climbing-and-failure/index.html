On Climbing and Failure

[July 4, 2018]

Categories: climbing Tags: failure bouldering

On Climbing and Failure

I’ve been ruminating on failure recently afer realizing I’m too cautious when climbing indoors1 and it may be stunting progress in a pretty non-trivial way.

Background

What kicked off this particular iteration of self-reflection was running into a friend at the bouldering gym with whom I lead-climb2 regularly. He was excited to show me his bouldering project which was a v9.3 Our skill levels are similar so I was surprised by the grade. Indoors, I typically flash4 v6 and v7’s tend to go5 within a couple tries, but I’ve only ever done a couple v8’s and I’d never even tried a v9. He talked me into trying his project. To my unmitigated surprise, I fell only at the last move. He showed me another v9 that he was working on. Again, I did way better than I expected.

The next time I went to the gym I got the first v9 and made progress on the second, getting to within spitting distance.

Failure Avoidance

Why had I never tried a v9 before? Part of it is certainly that I assumed they were too hard, but the more embarrassing part is that I was simple scared of failing. Why do we get scared of failing? Maybe it’s partly that failing will confirm the suspicion that you aren’t good enough. Maybe it’s fear of failing in front of your peers. The tech startup trope is to “fail fast” or celebrate failure, but it’s certainly easier said than done.

Playing it safe and only climbing things when I was confident of success has probably robbed me of progress for a long time.

Discovering and Surpassing Limits

Struggling promotes growth, easy success tends to just maintain the status quo.

This is immediately apparent for physical endeavours. You break down muscle so that it comes back stronger. It’s less obvious for mental endeavours, but imagine a programmer who only ever implemented the fibonnaci sequence or a merge-sort algorithm; small toys that they know they can write successfully. Or imagine a chess player who only ever challenges their immediate family. The lack of serious challenge will prevent any kind of progress.

Challenges without assured success reveal true limits and provide opportunities to surpass those limits. Maybe you succeed and have a new personal best or newfound confidence in yourself; at worst, these types of challenges provide a barometer for growth or a future goal.

Going forward

This experience has made me wonder whether self-doubt or handicapping has preventing me from growing in other disciplines. Going forward, when learning or trying to improve at some discipline, I’ll intentionally find projects or challenges that seem just out of reach and work on them anyways. I expect this will help paint a more accurate picture of my capabilities as well as help me get more comfortable with failing.
